Never underestimate the power of accessibility!

  • what accessibility is
  • why it matters

What is accessibility?

Accessibility involves a wide range of disabilities, including visual, auditory, physical, speech, cognitive, language, learning, and neurological disabilities.

Keep reading for information about Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)... spoiler: they are really important!

For many, "accessibility" sounds like hard work. Or at least, extra work. No-one likes that.
Accessibility is not an "add-on" or something that is "nice to have."
It's essential and should be at the front of anyone's mind when writing for the web.
These 6 reasons should be more than enough to convince you that it is worth it.

It is not about just a few individuals, it affects many of us.
An estimated 16.0 million people in the UK had a disability in 2021/22. This represents 24% of the total population.
Sometimes the challenges are not obvious and there are things that can make life easier for everyone.
Accessibility is about understanding that everyone is different and making sure technology works for all of us.

Accessibility helps reach more customers and builds trust.
When your site is accessible, it's easier for more people to use, which means a larger customer base.
It shows that your business cares about all customers, building trust and loyalty.
Accessibility can also lower legal risks and may improve your site's visibility in search engines.
It's a smart business move that leads to a broader audience, better user experiences, and improved results.

This a huge opportunity for businesses.
When businesses make things easy for everyone, they attract more customers.
More customers means more income.
So even if it costs time and money, accessibility is profitable in the long-run.

The internet is really important for people with disabilities, and it brings real advantages.
It’s a place where they can find information, connect with others, and do lots of things they couldn’t do before.
For those who face physical or sensory challenges, the web becomes a bridge to a more accessible world. It opens up a world of opportunities.
Embracing web accessibility isn’t just a choice; it’s a pathway to a fairer and more inclusive digital future where everyone can benefit.

The benefits of being accessible are not limited to the those with disabilities.
For example:
  • Closed captions were created to support people with visual impairments. They are useful in noisy and quiet settings. They improve comprehension, especially for non-native speakers.
  • Voice recognition technology was developed to support people with mobility or visual impairments. Now we can all benefit from hands-free control of devices.
  • High contrast themes and large text were designed for the visually impaired. They make it easier for everyone to read content in different lighting conditions and on different devices.
  • Alt text for images intended for screen readers helps search engines understand image content. It also gives contecxt to users when images fail to load.
  • The ability to zoom in on a map or magnify a font was created for people with low vision.
  • Keyboard navigation was designed for users who cannot use a mouse. Some people prefer to navigate websites without taking their hands off the keyboard. It’s also handy if your mouse breaks.
  • Simplified design, intended to support users with cognitive difficulties, improves usability for all. With less "noise", users can focus on understanding the content.
  • Text-to-speech was designed for the visually impaired. Now everyone can take in content passively, without having to read it themselves.
  • The average reading age of the UK adult population is 9 years old. So using simple words and shorter sentences is sensible. If you want to be understood.

Accessibility improves people’s lives.
This is the strongest argument for accessibility.
Perhaps it’s the only argument anyone needs to hear.
Think about this: blind people with access to a computer can now access almost every newspaper or magazine on their own.
Accessibility isn’t just a feature; it’s a fundamental human right that promotes inclusivity, equity, and dignity for everyone.

Accessibility helps everyone

Accessibility is about making sure that people with disabilities do not get left out.

How to make your content accessible

Long-term, use WCAG. Short-term, easy wins.

The official guidelines

If you are not already familiar with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), make it your mission to get familiar. Quickly.

Accessibility involves a wide range of disabilities, including visual, auditory, physical, speech, cognitive, language, learning, and neurological disabilities.

WCAG is a set of guidelines and standards developed by the Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) of W3C.

The primary purpose of WCAG is to make web content more accessible. The guidelines and success criteria are categorised into four principles:

  • Perceivable: Information and user interface components must be presented in a way that can be perceived by all users, regardless of their abilities.
  • Operable: User interface components and navigation must be operable by a wide range of users, including those with disabilities.
  • Understandable: Information and the operation of the user interface must be clear and understandable to all users.
  • Robust: Content must be robust enough to work with current and future technologies, ensuring its longevity and adaptability.

Quick wins

Here are some simple ways to make your existing content more accessible:

Alt text

Add appropriate alt text to every image.

Add an empty (or"null") alt text for images that screen readers should ignore.

Add helpful, descriptive text for the rest.

Use headings correctly.

Heading hierarchy helps people using screen readers to understand and navigate your content.

Use:

  • <h1> for the page title
  • <h2> for the major section headings
  • <h3> for sub-headings, and so on
Then use CSS to style as required.

Make forms work with screen readers

Use the HTML <label> element to link the fields with their text labels.

People using screen readers then know what they need to enter.

Be keyboard friendly

All content should be accessible by keyboard.

Not everyone can use a mouse.

Colour contrast matters

Colour contrast is crucial for anyone with limited eyesight or colour blindness.

Make sure there is a strong contrast between the text and background colours.

Remember: If someone can't read your content, it does not matter how "pretty" your site looks.

Give hyperlinks unique and descriptive names

Visually impaired users can use their screen reader to scan for links on a webpage.

So links are often read aloud without context.

Visit WCAG's Link Purpose page for more information.

Video and audio content

Turn off auto-play and enable closed captioning.

Automatic audio can be alarming and could overpower the voice of a screen reader.

Closed captioning and transcripts should also be available for the hearing impaired.

White space aka negative space

This reduces clutter and provides definition.

White space has been shown to make reading easier (for everyone).

It also improves understanding for those with cognitive difficulties..
