Writing for the web

It's not rocket surgery

This website is my work in progress guide to writing web content.

Maybe you're a blogger, own a small business, or just someone looking to share your passion.

As long as you are aiming to write for the web, you should – at the very least – find something useful on these pages.

So, let's dive in and explore the key principles and best practices for writing web content.

Uncover the core of great web content.
These 5 simple rules are the pillars that will set your content apart in the digital world.
Don't worry if they don't seem "simple" to begin with. They won't. They take practice.
Be patient and never expect to be perfect. Perfection is impossible.
Luckily, your users don't need you to be perfect.

Keep it simple. Don't hide what your users are looking for.
Write in short paragraphs. Usually only one or two sentences a paragraph.
People process information in chunks. Smaller chunks are easier to process.
Website users are not normally reading every word. They are looking for the answer to a question, or the next step in their journey.
Content with short paragraphs is easier to scan.
People:
  • are more likely to find what they are looking for
  • usually don’t mind scrolling to find what they are looking for
So even if it seems strange to you, writing in short paragraphs is usually best for your users.

Being concise means leaving out things that don’t help the user.
Concise writing is clear and does not include unnecessary or unclear words or language.
It can be tempting to make your writing sound more professional or academic by adding extra words. Resist the temptation.
Such "fluff" can be damaging for the user experience. It slows users down. It frustrates them. They may simply give up.
Focus on what the reader needs to know. No more, no less.
A word of caution: If you’re cutting your content because it’s too long, ask yourself:
  • Is this something the reader needs to know?
  • Is this the most concise way of saying it?

Being useful means that the information is of use to your audience.
This should be obvious, but... well, I can't be the only one to have experienced some pretty useless content.
Your job is to provide information that your users want to know.
This may even be boring content. User-friendly content often is.
It is not designed to entertain.
It is designed to help users solve their problem.
Remember: content is interesting to people if it is relevant to them.

Being consistent means using the same style and rules throughout your content.
This does not mean every page should be a carbon copy of the other.
It makes your website easy to understand and navigate.
When things look and work the same way every time, people can find what they need quickly, and they know what to expect.
It helps your website look professional and trustworthy, too.
Being consistent in web writing is like following a clear path, making the online experience better for everyone.

Make the user the hero of the story.
Bad content says what you want to say. It makes you the hero of the story.
Good content says what the user needs to know.
Always think about your users - what do they want to know?
Tell them that. Put yourself second.
Make the user the hero of your story.

Don't think of a pink elephant!

Things to be mindful of... so that you can avoid them

Personally, I don't like telling people what not to do. Mainly because it calls attention to it.
Forbidden things are tempting.
But there are various things that you may do without realising their consequences. So here's an overview of the beartraps and how to avoid them.

Avoid "click here"

Using "click here" for a link isn't helpful because it doesn't tell users where the link leads. Descriptive link text is better because it guides users and improves accessibility.

Link text should be descriptive

To write descriptive link text, use words that explain where the link will take you. This helps users understand the destination before clicking.

Avoid "see above"

Most users scan content. Telling them to look above – or below – means they have to go back over content.

It forces them to hunt for the relevant part.

Structure your content

Group information together.

You could use:

  • clear headings
  • short paragraphs
  • bulleted lists

If you do need to send the user away to the information, give them a link to follow.

Avoid Frequently Asked Questions (FAQS)

Bad content: making up some frequently asked questions and sticking them together in an unstructured list.

Answer those questions in your content

Good content: finding out what the most frequently asked questions really are, then putting the answers where people expect to find them.

Don't make people think

People want to find the right web page quickly and efficiently. If they have to stop and think along the way, it is not efficient or quick.

The smoothness of the path is more important than the number of clicks

The "3 click rule" is a myth.

If people are moving steadily on a successful path, they won't realise if it took them 4 or 5 clicks instead of 3.

Avoid "Please"

Please note that, the first three words of this sentence did nothing but slow you down.

Or perhaps it made you think I was going to tell you something important? Isn't all of the content important?

It hides what you are trying to say. It pushes meaningful words further down the sentence.

I love manners, but I'd recommend removing this word wherever you see it.

Be precise

Use clear and direct language to make requests or provide information.

For example:

  • Instead of "Please enter your name:"
  • Use: "Enter your name:"

Both are polite. The second is quicker and easier to read.

Avoid filler words

Example: "We will be happy to answer any questions you may have"

Ah, so you won't ask the questions that I don't have? Phew!

I have a list of word and phrases I want to remove from the internet, including:

  • "Future plans". They are just "plans"
  • "Utilise". You mean "use"
  • "What we've done in the past". The verb is already in the past tense. Say no more.
  • "Free gift". When is a gift not free?
Some may seem controversial, but I make no apologies!

Be direct

Krug's third law of usability: Get rid of half the words on each page. Then get rid of half of what's left.

3 key benefits of removing the waffle:

  • It reduces the noise level of the page
  • It makes the useful content more prominent
  • It makes pages shorter. Users can see more of the page at a glance without scrolling
